Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Information

About Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a condition where kidneys gradually lose their function over time. It can be caused by diabetes, high blood pressure, or other kidney-related diseases.

Management involves treating underlying conditions, making lifestyle changes, and in severe cases, dialysis or kidney transplantation may be necessary.

Symptoms of Chronic Kidney Disease

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Sleep problems
  • Changes in urination (more or less)
  • Decreased mental sharpness
  • Muscle cramps
  • Swelling of feet and ankles
  • Dry, itchy skin
  • Difficult to control high blood pressure
  • Shortness of breath (if fluid builds up in the lungs)
  • Chest pain (if fluid builds up around the heart)

These symptoms may develop gradually as kidney function declines.

When to See a Doctor

Make an appointment with your doctor if you experience any signs or symptoms of kidney disease. Early detection and treatment can help prevent kidney disease from progressing to kidney failure.

If you have risk factors for kidney disease, your doctor may monitor your blood pressure and kidney function with urine and blood tests during office visits.
